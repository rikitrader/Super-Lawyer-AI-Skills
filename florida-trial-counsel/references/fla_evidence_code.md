# Florida Evidence Code - Quick Reference

**Florida Statutes Chapter 90**

## General Provisions

### § 90.101 - Short Title
Chapter 90 may be cited as the "Florida Evidence Code."

### § 90.102 - Construction
These rules shall be construed to secure fairness, eliminate unjustifiable expense and delay, and promote the development of evidence law.

### § 90.103 - Scope; Applicability
The Florida Evidence Code applies to all civil actions, criminal proceedings, and court proceedings.

### § 90.104 - Rulings on Evidence

#### (a) Effect of Erroneous Ruling
Error may not be predicated upon a ruling unless a substantial right is affected.

#### (b) Objections
- **Objection required:** Timely objection stating specific ground
- **Offer of proof:** If evidence excluded, substance must be made known by offer

#### (c) Hearing Out of Jury's Presence
The court may conduct hearings on admissibility outside the jury's presence.

---

## Judicial Notice (§ 90.201-90.207)

### § 90.201 - Matters Subject to Judicial Notice
A court may take judicial notice of:
1. Florida law
2. Laws of United States and other states
3. Official actions of federal and state government
4. Rules of court
5. Domestic law

### § 90.202 - Matters Which Must Be Judicially Noticed
A court shall take judicial notice when requested by a party.

### § 90.203 - Compulsory Judicial Notice
When a party requests judicial notice and furnishes sufficient information, the court shall take judicial notice.

### § 90.204 - Determination of Propriety of Judicial Notice
The court shall take judicial notice at any stage of the proceeding.

### § 90.205 - Denial of Request
A court may decline to take judicial notice if insufficient information is provided.

### § 90.206 - Instructing Jury on Judicial Notice
The court shall instruct the jury that it may accept as established the matter judicially noticed.

### § 90.207 - Judicial Notice by Trial Court
In a civil action, the court shall instruct the jury to accept as conclusive any fact judicially noticed.

---

## Presumptions (§ 90.301-90.304)

### § 90.301 - Presumption Defined; Inferences
A presumption is an assumption of fact that the law requires to be made from another fact or group of facts found or otherwise established.

### § 90.302 - Classification of Rebuttable Presumptions
A rebuttable presumption shifts the burden of producing evidence.

### § 90.303 - Presumption Affecting Burden of Producing Evidence
A presumption affecting the burden of producing evidence requires the trier of fact to assume the existence of the presumed fact unless evidence is introduced sufficient to sustain a finding of its nonexistence.

### § 90.304 - Presumption Affecting Burden of Proof
A presumption affecting the burden of proof imposes upon the party against whom it operates the burden of proof as to the nonexistence of the presumed fact.

---

## Relevance (§ 90.401-90.410)

### § 90.401 - Definition of Relevant Evidence
"Relevant evidence" is evidence tending to prove or disprove a material fact.

### § 90.402 - Admissibility of Relevant Evidence
All relevant evidence is admissible, except as provided by law.

### § 90.403 - Exclusion on Grounds of Prejudice or Confusion
Relevant evidence is inadmissible if its probative value is substantially outweighed by:
1. The danger of unfair prejudice
2. Confusion of issues
3. Misleading the jury
4. Needless presentation of cumulative evidence

### § 90.404 - Character Evidence; When Admissible

#### (a) Character Evidence Generally
Evidence of a person's character is inadmissible to prove action in conformity therewith, except:
1. Character of accused (in criminal cases)
2. Character of victim (in criminal cases)
3. Character of witness

#### (b) Other Crimes, Wrongs, or Acts
Evidence of other crimes, wrongs, or acts is admissible when relevant to prove:
1. Motive
2. Opportunity
3. Intent
4. Preparation
5. Plan
6. Knowledge
7. Identity
8. Absence of mistake or accident

*Must give notice if to be used in criminal proceeding.*

### § 90.405 - Methods of Proving Character

#### (a) Reputation
Reputation evidence may be used.

#### (b) Specific Instances of Conduct
Specific instances may be proved when character is an essential element of a charge, claim, or defense.

### § 90.406 - Routine Practice of Organization
Evidence of routine practice is admissible to prove conduct on a particular occasion.

### § 90.407 - Subsequent Remedial Measures
Evidence of measures taken after an injury is not admissible to prove negligence or culpable conduct, but may be admissible for other purposes (impeachment, feasibility).

### § 90.408 - Compromise and Offers to Compromise
Evidence of furnishing, offering, or promising to furnish, or accepting, offering, or promising to accept a valuable consideration to compromise a claim is inadmissible to prove liability.

### § 90.409 - Payment of Medical and Similar Expenses
Evidence of furnishing, offering, or promising to pay medical expenses is not admissible to prove liability.

### § 90.410 - Offer to Plead Guilty; Nolo Contendere; Withdrawn Pleas of Guilty
Evidence of plea discussions is inadmissible.

## Privileges (§ 90.501-90.5105)

### § 90.501 - Privileges Recognized Only as Provided
Except as otherwise provided by statute, there is no privilege to refuse to be a witness or disclose any matter.

### § 90.502 - Lawyer-Client Privilege

#### (a) Definitions
- "Client" - person who consults a lawyer for legal services
- "Lawyer" - person authorized to practice law
- "Confidential" - not intended to be disclosed to third persons

#### (b) General Rule
A client has a privilege to refuse to disclose, and to prevent any other person from disclosing, confidential communications made for the purpose of facilitating the rendition of legal services.

#### (c) Who May Claim
The privilege may be claimed by:
1. The client
2. The client's guardian or conservator
3. The personal representative of a deceased client
4. The successor, trustee, or similar representative

#### (d) Exceptions
No privilege exists for:
1. Services to plan or commit crime or fraud
2. Claimants through same deceased client
3. Breach of duty by lawyer or client
4. Document attested by the lawyer
5. Joint clients

### § 90.503 - Psychotherapist-Patient Privilege
A patient has a privilege to refuse to disclose communications with a psychotherapist made for the purpose of diagnosis or treatment.

### § 90.504 - Husband-Wife Privilege

#### (a) Spousal Privilege
A spouse has a privilege during and after the marriage to refuse to disclose confidential communications.

#### (b) Exceptions
No privilege in:
1. Proceedings between the spouses
2. Crimes against spouse or child
3. Matters where both spouses are parties

### § 90.505 - Privilege with Respect to Communications to Clergy
A person has a privilege to refuse to disclose confidential communications made to a member of the clergy.

### § 90.506 - Accountant-Client Privilege
A client has a privilege to refuse to disclose confidential communications with an accountant made in the rendition of accounting services.

### § 90.507 - Waiver of Privilege by Voluntary Disclosure
A person who has a privilege waives the privilege if:
1. The person voluntarily discloses the matter
2. The person contracts with another to disclose

### § 90.508 - Privileged Matter Disclosed Under Compulsion or Without Opportunity to Claim Privilege
Evidence of a privileged communication is not admissible against the holder if disclosed under compulsion or without opportunity to claim the privilege.

### § 90.5105 - Fiduciary Lawyer-Client Privilege
The lawyer-client privilege applies to communications between a lawyer and a person in a fiduciary capacity.

## Witnesses (§ 90.601-90.616)

### § 90.601 - General Rule of Competency
Every person is competent to be a witness except as otherwise provided.

### § 90.603 - Disqualification of Witness
A person is disqualified to be a witness if:
1. Incapable of expressing themselves concerning the matter so as to be understood
2. Incapable of understanding the duty of a witness to tell the truth

### § 90.604 - Lack of Personal Knowledge
A witness may not testify to a matter unless evidence is introduced sufficient to support a finding that the witness has personal knowledge.

### § 90.605 - Oath or Affirmation of Witness
Before testifying, a witness shall be required to declare by oath or affirmation that the testimony will be truthful.

### § 90.606 - Interpreters and Translators
An interpreter or translator shall take an oath to make a true interpretation or translation.

### § 90.607 - Competency of Certain Persons as Witnesses

#### (a) General Rule
Except as provided below, no person is disqualified from being a witness.

#### (b) Dead Man's Statute
In civil actions, no person interested in the event shall testify to any communication or transaction between the person and the deceased or incapacitated person, except:
1. When corroborating witness testifies
2. When deceased's representative opens the door
3. In certain other circumstances

### § 90.608 - Who May Impeach
Any party may attack the credibility of a witness, including a party's own witness.

### § 90.609 - Character of Witness as Impeachment
The credibility of a witness may be attacked by evidence that the witness's character for truthfulness is bad.

### § 90.610 - Conviction of Certain Crimes as Impeachment
A party may attack the credibility of a witness by evidence of conviction of a crime if:
1. The crime was punishable by death or imprisonment exceeding 1 year
2. The crime involved dishonesty or false statement

### § 90.611 - Religious Beliefs or Opinions
Evidence of a witness's beliefs on matters of religion is inadmissible to attack or support credibility.

### § 90.612 - Mode and Order of Interrogation and Presentation

#### (a) Court Control
The court shall exercise control over the mode and order of interrogating witnesses.

#### (b) Cross-Examination
Cross-examination shall be limited to the subject matter of direct examination and matters affecting credibility.

#### (c) Leading Questions
Leading questions should not be used on direct examination except as necessary for development of testimony.

### § 90.613 - Refreshing the Memory of a Witness
A witness may use a writing or other item to refresh memory while testifying.

### § 90.614 - Prior Statements of Witnesses

#### (a) Examining Witness About Prior Statement
Before a prior inconsistent statement may be offered in evidence, the witness must be told the contents, time, place, and person to whom made.

#### (b) Extrinsic Evidence
Extrinsic evidence of a prior inconsistent statement is admissible only if the witness has been examined about the statement.

### § 90.615 - Calling Witnesses by the Court
The court may call witnesses on its own motion and interrogate witnesses.

### § 90.616 - Exclusion of Witnesses
At the request of a party, the court shall exclude witnesses from the courtroom so they cannot hear the testimony of other witnesses.

## Opinion Evidence and Expert Testimony (§ 90.701-90.706)

### § 90.701 - Opinion Testimony of Lay Witnesses
A witness not testifying as an expert may testify in the form of an opinion when:
1. Rationally based on the perception of the witness
2. Helpful to understanding the testimony or determining a fact

### § 90.702 - Testimony by Experts
If scientific, technical, or other specialized knowledge will assist the trier of fact, a witness qualified as an expert may testify in the form of an opinion if:
1. The testimony is based upon sufficient facts or data
2. The testimony is the product of reliable principles and methods
3. The witness has applied the principles and methods reliably to the facts

*Florida follows the Daubert standard for expert testimony.*

### § 90.703 - Opinion on Ultimate Issue
Testimony in the form of an opinion is not objectionable because it embraces an ultimate issue.

### § 90.704 - Basis of Opinion Testimony by Experts
Facts or data upon which an expert bases an opinion need not be admissible in evidence if reasonably relied upon by experts in the field.

### § 90.705 - Disclosure of Facts or Data Underlying Expert Opinion
The expert may testify in terms of opinion and give reasons without first testifying to the underlying facts. The expert may be required to disclose those facts on cross-examination.

### § 90.706 - Authoritativeness of Literature for Use in Cross-Examination
A published treatise, periodical, or pamphlet may be used in cross-examination of an expert if recognized as authoritative.

## Hearsay (§ 90.801-90.806)

### § 90.801 - Hearsay; Definitions; Exceptions

#### (a) Statement
A "statement" is an oral or written assertion or nonverbal conduct intended as an assertion.

#### (b) Declarant
A "declarant" is a person who makes a statement.

#### (c) Hearsay
"Hearsay" is a statement, other than one made by the declarant while testifying at trial or hearing, offered to prove the truth of the matter asserted.

#### (d) Statements Not Hearsay
A statement is not hearsay if:
1. **Prior statement by witness:** Prior inconsistent statement given under oath, prior consistent statement to rebut charge of improper influence, or statement of identification
2. **Admission by party-opponent:** Statement by the party or authorized agent

### § 90.802 - Hearsay Rule
Hearsay is inadmissible except as provided by statute.

### § 90.803 - Hearsay Exceptions; Availability of Declarant Immaterial

The following are not excluded by the hearsay rule, even if the declarant is available:

#### (1) Spontaneous Statement
A spontaneous statement describing or explaining an event or condition made while the declarant was perceiving the event or immediately thereafter.

#### (2) Excited Utterance
A statement relating to a startling event or condition made while the declarant was under the stress of excitement caused by the event.

#### (3) Then-Existing Mental, Emotional, or Physical Condition
A statement of the declarant's then-existing state of mind, emotion, sensation, or physical condition (motive, intent, plan, pain, bodily health), but not including memory or belief to prove the fact remembered.

#### (4) Statements for Purposes of Medical Diagnosis or Treatment
Statements for purposes of medical diagnosis or treatment describing medical history, past or present symptoms, pain, or their cause, insofar as reasonably pertinent to diagnosis or treatment.

#### (5) Recorded Recollection
A memorandum or record concerning a matter about which a witness once had knowledge but now has insufficient recollection, shown to have been made when the matter was fresh in the witness's memory.

#### (6) Records of Regularly Conducted Business Activity
A memorandum, report, record, or data compilation made:
1. At or near the time
2. By a person with knowledge
3. Kept in the course of a regularly conducted business activity
4. As a regular practice of the business

*Custodian or qualified witness may authenticate. Certification permitted.*

#### (7) Absence of Entry in Records of Regularly Conducted Activity
Evidence that a matter is not included in the records of a regularly conducted activity, to prove the nonoccurrence of the matter.

#### (8) Public Records and Reports
Records, reports, statements, or data compilations of public offices setting forth:
1. Activities of the office
2. Matters observed pursuant to duty
3. Factual findings from authorized investigations

*In civil actions, factual findings from investigations are admissible.*

#### (9) Records of Vital Statistics
Records of births, deaths, marriages if the report was required by law.

#### (10) Absence of Public Record or Entry
To prove the absence of a record or entry normally made by a public office.

#### (11) Records of Religious Organizations
Statements in a regularly kept record of a religious organization.

#### (12) Marriage, Baptismal, and Similar Certificates
Statements in a certificate or similar document.

#### (13) Family Records
Statements of fact concerning personal or family history in family Bibles, genealogies, charts, etc.

#### (14) Records of Documents Affecting Interest in Property
Records of a document purporting to establish or affect interest in property.

#### (15) Statements in Documents Affecting Interest in Property
Statements in a document affecting interest in property if the matter stated was relevant.

#### (16) Statements in Ancient Documents
Statements in a document in existence 20 years or more.

#### (17) Market Reports, Commercial Publications
Market quotations, tabulations, lists, directories, or other published compilations generally used and relied upon by the public.

#### (18) Learned Treatises
Statements in published treatises, periodicals, or pamphlets on a subject of history, medicine, or other science or art, established as a reliable authority.

#### (19) Reputation Concerning Personal or Family History
Reputation among family members.

#### (20) Reputation Concerning Boundaries or General History
Reputation in a community concerning boundaries, customs, events of general history.

#### (21) Reputation as to Character
Reputation of a person's character.

#### (22) Former Testimony
Testimony given as a witness at another hearing if the party against whom the testimony is offered had an opportunity to develop the testimony by examination.

#### (23) Hearsay Exception; Statement of Child Victim
Out-of-court statements by child victims of abuse are admissible under certain conditions.

#### (24) Hearsay Exception; Statement of Elderly Person or Disabled Adult
Similar exception for elderly or disabled adult victims.

### § 90.804 - Hearsay Exceptions; Declarant Unavailable

#### (a) Definition of Unavailability
Unavailability includes situations in which the declarant:
1. Is exempted by privilege
2. Refuses to testify despite court order
3. Lacks memory
4. Is dead or unable to testify due to physical or mental illness
5. Is absent and the proponent cannot procure attendance

#### (b) Hearsay Exceptions
When the declarant is unavailable:

##### (1) Former Testimony
Testimony given at another hearing if the party had opportunity for examination.

##### (2) Statement Under Belief of Impending Death
A statement made by a declarant while believing that death was imminent, concerning the cause or circumstances.

##### (3) Statement Against Interest
A statement that was against the declarant's pecuniary, proprietary, or penal interest when made.

##### (4) Statement of Personal or Family History
Statements concerning the declarant's own birth, adoption, marriage, or other similar facts.

##### (5) Statement by Deceased or Ill Declarant
When the declarant is deceased or unable to testify, a statement describing or explaining an event or condition made within 24 months before filing of the action.

### § 90.805 - Hearsay Within Hearsay
Hearsay within hearsay is not excluded if each part of the combined statements conforms to an exception.

### § 90.806 - Attacking and Supporting Credibility of Declarant
The credibility of the declarant may be attacked and, if attacked, supported, as if the declarant testified.

## Authentication (§ 90.901-90.903)

### § 90.901 - Requirement of Authentication or Identification
Authentication or identification is required as a condition precedent to admissibility.

### § 90.902 - Self-Authentication
Extrinsic evidence of authenticity is not required for:
1. Domestic public documents under seal
2. Domestic public documents not under seal (certified)
3. Foreign public documents
4. Certified copies of public records
5. Official publications
6. Newspapers and periodicals
7. Trade inscriptions and labels
8. Acknowledged documents
9. Commercial paper
10. Business records accompanied by certification

### § 90.903 - Subscribing Witness's Testimony Unnecessary
Testimony of a subscribing witness is not necessary unless required by statute.

## Best Evidence Rule (§ 90.951-90.958)

### § 90.951 - Definitions
- "Writings and recordings" include letters, words, numbers, or their equivalent.
- "Photographs" include still photographs, X-ray films, video tapes, motion pictures.
- "Original" means the writing or recording itself.
- "Duplicate" means a counterpart produced by the same impression.

### § 90.952 - Requirement of Originals
To prove the content of a writing, recording, or photograph, the original is required except as otherwise provided.

### § 90.953 - Admissibility of Duplicates
A duplicate is admissible to the same extent as the original unless:
1. A genuine question exists as to authenticity
2. It would be unfair to admit the duplicate

### § 90.954 - Admissibility of Other Evidence of Contents
Other evidence of contents is admissible if:
1. Originals lost or destroyed (not in bad faith)
2. Original not obtainable
3. Original in possession of opponent
4. Collateral matter

### § 90.955 - Public Records
Contents of a public record may be proved by a copy certified as correct.

### § 90.956 - Summaries
Contents of voluminous writings may be presented in the form of a chart, summary, or calculation.

### § 90.957 - Testimony or Written Admission of Party
Contents of writings may be proved by testimony or written admission of the party against whom offered.

### § 90.958 - Functions of Court and Jury
The court determines preliminary questions; the jury determines weight and credibility.
